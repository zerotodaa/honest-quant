The trap

A leveraged crypto strategy backtested beautifully: 78 of 140 trades green, a clean equity curve, a healthy-looking Sharpe. Out-of-sample splits passed. The p-values looked significant. Everything a normal backtest checks said ship it.

It was a mirage. On a larger, truly independent sample of 438 trades, the same rules reversed to -$974. The "edge" was small-sample overfitting — and the standard guards (a single OOS split, nominal p-values) were fooled right along with it.

Why it fooled the usual checks

  • Small-sample luck looks identical to skill until the sample gets big.
  • Multiple testing: try enough configs and the best one is noise dressed as alpha.
  • One OOS split isn't enough — and a raw Sharpe says nothing once you account for how many things you tried.

What I build instead — an honest-evaluation gate

I treat "does this edge survive?" as the deliverable, not the pretty equity curve. The gate I build into every system:

  • Nested cross-validation with a leakage guard — tune only on inner folds, score once on untouched data.
  • Deflated & Probabilistic Sharpe (PSR/DSR) — corrects the Sharpe for the number of configs tried and for fat tails.
  • Probability of Backtest Overfitting (PBO) via combinatorial CV.
  • A 300+ independent-sample gate — no edge is "real" until it survives one.
  • Realistic fills, slippage, funding, and liquidation modeling — most backtest engines silently skip funding and never liquidate, so they overstate every leveraged result.

Proof it isn't just theory

I recently rebuilt a Binance BTC perpetual strategy on a clean event-driven engine with this gate wired in. The verdict it returned: NO ROBUST EDGE AFTER DEFLATION — PBO 0.59, only 79 independent trades (below the 300 gate). The gate blocked promotion to live. That is the system working: it refused to risk real money on a number that looked good and wasn't.

A losing backtest you trusted costs you everything. A winning backtest you didn't verify costs you more. The value is the verification.
